Canberra's most haunted places
National Film and Sound Archive

Australian Institute of Anatomy

Described by some as "one of Australia's most haunted buildings", the National Film and Sound Archive used to be home to the Australian Institute of Anatomy. Its basement contained a morgue, and skeletons used to be on display for attendees to inspect.

Hotel Kurrajong

1951

Hotel Kurrajong has been home to many political figures over the years, none more prominent than Prime Minister Ben Chiffley. One evening in 1951, the then-Prime Minister suffered a fatal heart attack while working in his room — and some say a part of him never left.

National Museum of Australia

Canberra Hospital

By far the newest and least obviously creepy building on this list — the National Museum might seem benign, but according to Montgomery it harbours a secret straight out of The Shining.

"There are numerous stories there of a ghostly matron that seemed to be attached to the old hospital"

Blundells Cottage

1892

Blundells Cottage sits on the shores of Lake Burley Griffin, beneath the looming ASIO headquarters, so it's spooky in more ways than one. According to Montgomery, items move about inside, and some visitors smell burning flesh — a throwback to the tragic death of a teenage girl more than a century ago.

National Film and Sound Archive

The national fim and sound archive is describe to be the most haunted buildings in Australia. It used to be the home of the Australian Institute of Anatomy, for more than 50 years. The Australian Institute of Anatomy used to be house human skeletons, animal specimens and artefacts, and the site of scientific experiments on display.
Another of the commonly reported ghost sightings is that of a little girl that would pop out through a grate in the old theatrette and make visiting school students laugh. There have also been reports of poltergeist activity, particularly where the dissection laboratories used to be. Since the NFSA moved in, that space has been used as an office with two sound recording booths. "Quite often staff would have meetings in that room, and they would hear noises coming from the [recording booths] and they would see things flying around in there," Tim the Yowie Man said. "All these tapes had fallen out of anti-gravity tape decks, which can't happen unless someone or something had forced them out."

However, it was one particular Labor politician that gave the hotel its most notorious incident. Ben Chifley lived at Hotel Kurrajong in Room 205 for eleven years. When he became Prime Minister in 1945, he refused to live in the Lodge, despite it being the official residence, and instead continued to live in his room. He was often spotted on the porch of the Hotel, filling his pipe and smoking. He walked to and from Old Parliament House every day.
